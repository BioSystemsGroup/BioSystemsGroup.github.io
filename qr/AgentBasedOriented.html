Agent-Based & Agent Oriented

Pervasive uncertainty, sparse system information, and considerable variability make distinguishing causes from effects difficult. Agent-based methods provide the flexibility, extensibility, and generality needed to assemble software analog mechanisms that become increasingly biomimetic during execution. A liver lobule and the ISL analog are similarly compartmentalized.

Many biological processes are analogized as logical statements; for instance, if protein A binds to protein B; then protein B is activated. Our agents implement similar rule-based behavior when mediating interacting components.

A goal of these experiments is that ISL measurements made at different spatial and temporal scales during the same simulation can be mapped separately and quantitatively to wet-lab measurements also spanning different spatial and temporal scales. Achieving that goal requires relational grounding throughout the analog. Consequently, specific mapping models are expected to vary. For more information see http://www.tbiomed.com/content/8/1/35.

The variety of analog-to-wet-lab mappings (i.e. the comparison of analog to referent attributes) is a direct measure of an analog's multiscaleness. A mapping has a spatial and temporal scale. Biological spatial scales typically correspond to functional "levels" being observed. A wet-lab experiment's temporal scales typically correspond to sets of measurements at intervals. Likewise, the state of different analog components (often at different spatial scales) updates at different time steps/simulation cycles.
